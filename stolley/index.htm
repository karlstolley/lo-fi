Preamble

The days of designing webpages to be webpages have ended. So have the days of using WYSIWYG editors, which misguidedly incorporate web authors’ visual choices, making them a part of the structural code (XHTML) or HTML.

But a casual observer of our field would never know this, given that writing for the Web is often taught like it’s 1998.

Web production now stands on the threshold of a new era, the era of the semantic Web (which will soon be so synonymous with digital discourse as to be a redundant term). This is an era of XML and structural documents, an era of increasingly diverse and ubiquitous web access that goes far beyond the familiar portal of the desktop browser (which is what one purportedly “sees” in a WYSIWYG editor). This is an era when devices will need to be able to interpret webpages and web-available discourse as much as, if not more than, humans.

How will we, in the loosely defined fields of rhetoric and composition, computers and writing, and technical communication, prepare our discourse–and teach our students–to participate and even shape the direction of digital discourse?

We will not rely on software. Dreamweaver will not save us, nor Frontpage. Nor will Drupal, nor any other piece of software–open-source or otherwise–so long as that software protects us from the deeper levels of a document’s structural meaning (and the interdependent levels of visual presentation across a variety of devices and media), and so long as software insists on making visual choices (“what you see”) the primary mode of web development. No, this software will not save us–in fact, it will only harm the progress and improvement, the flexibility and sustainability of web-available discourse.

Definitions

L.O.-F.I.

  • Lossless: Discourse presented through lo-fi production technologies neither degrades nor becomes trapped in the production itself. Text migrates and transforms from a single source; images, video, and other media maintain their integrity as individual files.
  • Open: Lo-fi production technologies make their source code and materials available for inspection, revision, and extension outside of the scope of any one piece of production software. This includes, by default, end-user/reader customization and repurposing. Aside from single-media files for image, video, and sound, everything about a digital artifact: its structure, its visual presentation, and any interactive elements, including motion, should be described in source code.
  • Flexible: Content presented using lo-fi production technologies can be experienced unobstrusively in different ways by different users.
  • In(ter)dependent: Lo-fi production technologies direct orchestration (a recipe), not composition (a TV dinner), allowing users’ devices to interpret the production the way a chef interprets a recipe
